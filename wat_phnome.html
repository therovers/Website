Wat Phnom, Phnom Penh, Cambodia

Home

“Buddhaṃ śaraṇaṃ gacchāmi. Dharmaṃ śaraṇaṃ gacchāmi. Sanghaṃ śaraṇaṃ gacchāmi.”

These holy verses of Buddhism echoes in one’s mind while visiting such a great temple of Buddha-Wat Phnom. Wat Phnom (Mountain Pagoda) is a sacred Buddhist pagoda located in Phnom Penh, Cambodia. It is the tallest religious structure in the city as it stands on a small hill. The pagoda was earlier known by the name of Wat Preah Chedey Borapaut.

Location: Phnom Penh, Cambodia Denomination: Theravada Buddhism (a branch of Buddhism which follows the teachings of the Pali Canon) Founded: Built in 1373. The sanctuary was rebuilt several times in the 19th century and again in 1926. History: The legend of the founding of Wat Phnom tells of a local wealthy widow, named Lady Penh ( commonly referred as Daun Penh- Grandmother Penh- in Khmer) living at Chaktomuk, found a large koki tree in the river in 1373. Inside the tree she found four bronze statues of Buddha. Daun Penh began to build up a low mound of earth in the land close to her home and constructed it into a manmade hill. Eventually she constructed a small shrine on an artificial hill made with the help of the local people to protect the sacred statues.  With time this became a sacred site and sanctuary where people would make blessings and pray.  Thus, the name of the Capital of Cambodia came to be known as Phnom Penh- the Mountain of Daun Penh. Another account of the founding of the Wat suggests King Ponhea Yat (r. 1405-67), built the sanctuary (vihara) when he moved his capital from Angkor to Phnom Penh in 1422. King Ponhea Yat ordered His Excellency Decho Srei to raise the mount even higher when he finished building the new Royal Palace in the new city and constructed a sanctuary at the same place.   Style: The style used is Khmer style of architecture, having sloping roof and high gable, with designs having Khmer touch.

Art:

  1. The inner wall of the pagoda is decorated with murals related to mythology.
  2. The murals depict stories from the Jataka (Buddha's earlier reincarnations before his enlightenment).
  3. Vessantara Jataka (Great Birth Sermon- one the most popular avadanas of Theravada Budhhism) murals also adorn the walls of the pagoda. 
  4. There are also murals depicting stories from the Reamker, the Khmer version of the Ramayana.
  5. The newer murals in the bottom tiers have both traditional and modern style.

Architecture:

  1. It stands 27 metres (88.5 ft) above the ground.
  2. The complex faces toward the East. The main entrance is via a broad grand staircase, which is guarded by guardians, lions and naga (mythical serpent) balustrades.
  3. At the main stairway leading to the Pagoda, is the Statue of King Sisowath (King of Cambodia from 1904 to 1927) with attendants.
  4. West of the vihara is a huge stupa containing the ashes of the King and the royal family.
  5. The southwest corner of the vihara and stupa, is a small shrine dedicated to Lady Penh. Items used in daily life such as teapot set, makeup items, flowers, ornaments and even water bottles are kept near the statue of Lady Penh. 
  6. The interior has a central altar complex with a large bronze seated Buddha surrounded by other statues, flowers, candles and items of devotion and worship.
  7.  To the north of the vihara and below it is a shrine dedicated to the genie Preah Chau, who is especially revered by the Vietnamese.
  8. There is a giant flower clock in the front of the Wat. It is one of the temple's most striking visual elements. It is nearly 20 meter width.  The original clock was from France and it was installed in 1960.  But in the year 2000 it was replaced by a new version as a gift from China to mark the friendship between the two countries. The clock plays the Cambodian national anthem twice a day during sunrise and sunset.

Other features:

  1. Wat Phnom is the center of celebration during Khmer New Year and Pchum Ben (festival of the Dead which is an animist type of ancestor worship or respect offerings).
  2. At the front gable there is a statue of Indra Deva on three headed elephant Airawat.
  3. At front there are relief work resembling those in Angkor Wat.
  4. Inside the main Wat there is a statue of Daun Penh, draped in fine cloth and regularly supplied wih cosmetics, all the things supposed to be liked by Grandma Penh.
  5. One of the statues which can easily be noticed inside the Wat is of Phra Mae Torani holding her hair. According to Buddhist myths, Phra Mae Torani is the personified form of Earth Mother who released cool waters out of her hair to drown Mara, the demon who wanted to stop Buddha from getting enlightenment while Buddha was meditating under the Bodhi tree.
  6. Inside the temple there are several statues of Buddha in Bhumisparsha mudra, Reclined Buddha and Buddha holding a bowl.
  7. Lotus flowers, candles, coconut water and incense sticks are a popular offering in the temple.
  8. Wat Phnom appeared on the Travel Channel documentary, 1,000 Places to See Before You Die.
